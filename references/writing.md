# Ori Writing Rules

Design carries authority only if the words deserve it. These are the
content quality bars per artifact. The universal bar: every sentence must
survive "so what?" — if a reader could delete it without losing a decision
input, delete it first.

## Universal rules

- **Assertions over topics.** Headings state findings, not categories.
  "Gen-Z trusts creators over brands 3:1", not "Trust metrics".
- **Numbers do the arguing.** Prefer one sourced number to three adjectives.
  Round to meaningful precision (47.3% → 47% unless the decimal matters).
- **Verb-led bullets**, one line each. If a bullet wraps, cut words until it
  doesn't.
- **No throat-clearing.** Delete "In today's fast-paced world", "It's
  important to note", "This document outlines". Open with the finding.
- **Honest provenance.** Simulated-audience findings are labeled as
  simulated. Unverifiable numbers become magnitudes or get cut. No invented
  testimonials, logos, or "trusted by" walls.
- **Sentence case everywhere** — headings, labels excepted (mono labels are
  uppercase by design).
- Spell out an acronym at first use unless the audience dimension says
  expert.

## One-pager

- The metric row IS the argument: choose the 3–4 numbers that tell the
  story; everything else supports them.
- Lede ≤ 2 sentences: what this is, what you're asking for.
- Each section ≤ 90 words. End with a callout carrying the ask or decision.
- If content wants a second page, the one-pager is doing a long doc's job —
  say so and switch templates.

## Long doc

- Cover states the claim of the whole document in one display line, not the
  topic.
- Every chapter opens with its conclusion in the first paragraph, then
  supports it.
- One figure per page max; each figure referenced in prose ("Fig 03 shows…").
- Executive summary ≤ 1 page, written last, readable standalone.

## Report (insight report)

- Section = claim (H2) + evidence (one shape) + insight strip ("SO WHAT").
- The insight strip states an implication or recommended action, never a
  restatement of the chart.
- Segment findings quote personas by name; quotes ≤ 25 words, verbatim.
- Methods and sample sizes live in a final `METHOD` callout — small, honest,
  present.
- Lead the report with the variant finding: what the reader doesn't already
  believe.

## Slides

- Ghost test: titles alone, read in order, are the complete pitch.
- ≤ 24 words of body text per slide beyond the title. Slides are evidence
  displays, not documents.
- Takeaway bar text ≤ 14 words, states the "therefore".
- Context slot holds the one number the title claims, or a scope kicker
  (who/what/when, ≤ 15 words) — never a second sentence of argument.
- Numbers on slides get magnitude context ("2.4× baseline", not "2.4×").
- Closing slide: one ask, one contact line. No "Thank you" slide with
  nothing on it.

## Resume

- Every bullet: **Action + Scope + Result + Business outcome.**
  "Rebuilt ingest pipeline (Action) across 6 data sources (Scope), cutting
  p95 latency 70% (Result) and unblocking enterprise launch (Outcome)."
- Quantify or cut: bullets without a number or concrete artifact are
  suspects.
- Summary ≤ 3 lines, no adjectives about personality ("passionate",
  "driven") — evidence implies them.
- Skills section: nouns only, grouped, no proficiency bars ever.
- Reverse chronology; gaps unexplained are fine; page 2 only if the last 5
  years alone overflow page 1.

## Landing page

- Hero H1 ≤ 9 words, states the outcome the visitor buys, not the feature.
- Subline ≤ 20 words, concrete mechanism ("Simulate your audience before
  you ship the campaign").
- Every section earns scroll: benefit → proof → mechanism → CTA rhythm.
- Social proof must be real; if none exists yet, use a concrete product
  fact instead of fake logos.
- CTA verbs are specific: "Simulate your audience", not "Get started".
- Microcopy under CTA answers the top objection (price, setup time,
  data privacy).
